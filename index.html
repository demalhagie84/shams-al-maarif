الكتاب

The Book Behind the Rumors

four parts · forty chapters · and an honest account of where it came from

The Shams al-Maʿārif al-Kubrā is the great compendium of Islamic letter-science: the twenty-eight letters and their four natures, the Ninety-Nine Names, the seven planetary squares, the lunar mansions, the hours and their rulers — forty chapters moving from the alphabet to the architecture of the cosmos. The copy behind this edition is the four-parts-in-one-volume printing of Muṣṭafā al-Bābī al-Ḥalabī, Cairo, completed Rajab 1345 AH (1927 CE) — 575 numbered pages and a sixteen-page fihrist, corrected for the press by Ibrāhīm ibn Ḥasan al-Anbābī, with two treatises on the zodiac and the wandering stars bound in at the end. It last left a library desk in March 1991; it opens here for good.

As a genre, historians of Islamic esotericism catalog their contents under headings such as taskhīr (claims of subjugating spirits), ʿazāʾim (conjuration formulae), aggressive and defensive khawāṣṣ (claimed properties turned toward compelling, binding, separating, or shielding), and amulet-craft hedged with elaborate ritual conditions. This edition describes those categories the way a museum catalog describes a blade — provenance, form, and history, never the edge: no procedure, formula, timing, or condition from these chapters appears anywhere on this site. The part closes with the two bound treatises and the fihrist.
Who wrote it? — the honest answer

The book travels under the name of Aḥmad ibn ʿAlī al-Būnī (d. c. 622 AH / 1225 CE), the Maghribī master of the science of letters. But modern manuscript scholarship is clear: the Kubrā as it circulates today is a later compilation that grew around al-Būnī's authentic works for centuries after his death — no known manuscript of this long recension predates the 11th/17th century. Genuine Būnian material runs through it, braided with additions no one signed. This edition tells you so plainly, because reverence for a text includes telling the truth about it — the same taṣḥīḥ ethic that governs our sister edition.

The Sealed Chamber — what this edition will not do

The Kubrā contains extensive operative material: procedures aimed at compelling, binding, separating, and harming. None of it is reproduced, summarized, or operationalized here — not the recipes, not the timings, not the conditions. The sword-chapters are treated the way a museum treats a blade: studied for structure and history behind glass, never handed across the counter. What is opened is everything the arithmetic can prove and everything a student of the tradition can licitly study: letters, numbers, names, squares, and the sky's calendar.

الفصل الأول

The Twenty-Eight Letters

four natures · seven letters each · tap any letter — toggle the two reckonings

The book opens where the tradition always opens: the alphabet as cosmology. Twenty-eight letters — the number of the mansions of the moon — divided four by seven among fire, air, water, and earth. Al-Būnī wrote in the Maghreb, where several letters carry different values than in the East; this edition defaults to the Mashriqī reckoning used across our sister edition, with the Maghribī a single tap away — because a verified edition shows its arithmetic in both dialects.

المنازل

The Letters and the Mansions

28 letters · 28 stations of the moon · tap any bead on the wheel

The correspondence the whole book stands on: the moon crosses one manzila — one mansion of fixed stars — each night, completing the circle of twenty-eight in a lunar month, and the tradition pairs each station with one of the twenty-eight letters in abjad order, alif with the first mansion through ghayn with the last. A calendar made of language; an alphabet paced by the sky. The wheel below turns the pairing into an instrument — each bead colored by its letter's element.

آية الكرسي

The Throne Verse

al-Baqara 255 · the book's crown of protective recitation · devotional study

In its own chapter the book treats this verse as the summit of protective recitation, and centuries of devotional practice agree. Read as the tradition reads it, the verse moves through a single sustained declaration: the Living, the Self-Subsisting One whom no drowsiness or sleep ever touches; the sole owner of whatever the heavens and the earth contain; before whom no intercession moves without leave; who encompasses what creatures face and what lies behind them, while they grasp of His knowledge only what He wills; whose Footstool embraces the heavens and the earth together, their keeping no weight upon Him — the High, the Tremendous. This edition presents it as the book's devotional center of gravity: a text for recitation and reflection, whole and unadorned.

الرسالتان

The Two Bound Treatises

the sky-lore appended to the printing · presented as history of science

The 1345 AH printing closes with two short rasāʾil of classical astrology: Zahr al-murūj fī dalāʾil al-burūj — “The Flower of the Meadows, on the Indications of the Signs” — on the twelve zodiacal signs, and Laṭāʾif al-ishāra fī khaṣāʾiṣ al-kawākib al-sayyāra — “The Subtleties of Indication, on the Characters of the Wandering Stars” — on the temperaments of the seven planets. What follows is that sky-lore in summary, described as the tradition described it: the historical worldview the book's readers carried, presented for study rather than belief or practice.
